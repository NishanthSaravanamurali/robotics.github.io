Forward Kinematics

Forward kinematics involves calculating the position and orientation of the robot's end-effector given the joint parameters. It uses the known joint angles and link lengths to determine the end-effector's location in space.

  • Input: Joint parameters (angles for revolute joints and displacements for prismatic joints)
  • Output: Position and orientation of the end-effector

The process involves using the Twist system parameters (in our robot) and transformation matrices to systematically compute the position of each link relative to the base frame. The final transformation matrix represents the pose of the end-effector.

Clarifying Linear vs. Angular Velocity

  • Linear Velocity: This is the rate at which an object moves along a straight path in space. It is measured in units of distance per time (e.g., meters per second) and has three components corresponding to movement along the x, y, and z axes.
  • Angular Velocity: This is the rate at which an object rotates around a point or axis. It is measured in units of angle per time (e.g., radians per second) and has three components corresponding to rotation about the x, y, and z axes.

Inverse Kinematics

Inverse kinematics involves determining the joint parameters that achieve a desired position and orientation of the robot's end-effector. It is often more complex than forward kinematics due to the possibility of multiple solutions or no solution at all.

  • Input: Desired position and orientation of the end-effector
  • Output: Joint parameters needed to achieve that position and orientation

Inverse kinematics can be solved using analytical or numerical methods. Analytical methods provide a closed-form solution but are only feasible for simpler robots. Numerical methods, such as iterative algorithms, are more versatile but may require good initial guesses and can be computationally intensive.

Challenges in inverse kinematics include dealing with singularities, where the robot's movement becomes indeterminate, and ensuring that the solutions are within the robot's physical constraints.
